Getting to Know and Understand Boxers

The first step in the process was to get to know the users. This involved going on trips to boxing gyms and meeting users in their environment. We listened to stories, observed their daily routine, and tried to determine their guiding values and beliefs. We recorded all this information through pictures and careful notetaking.

After many discussions on insights gained from our user visits, we developed personas and areas of opportunity. These frameworks also helped us get deeper into why our users did the things they did, something we had trouble with initially.

Idea Generation and Co-Designing

Using our areas of opportunity and personas, we came up with as many ideas as we could. These ideas covered a range of feasibility, and looked to help recreational boxers in many different aspects of their lives.

From there, we took some of these basic concepts to our users for their help in shaping them. As you can see above, the progression of our ideas wasn’t very linear. Some ideas would be combined, or altered based on our codesigns.

Codesigning with props were a challenge for our team, because we found it hard to push our users to imagine our sketch models as final products. While we did use props in our codesign, we also got a lot of feedback through various other methods.

Finally, we were left with 3 ideas that we thought could make the most impact in our users’ lives.

Developing the Product into a Concept

To develop the concept, we had to think specifically about how the user would interact with our product. To do this, we used a variety of frameworks. One of the more useful frameworks was the Function, Interaction, Character framework. It helped us organize our thoughts on how the various parts of the product would work together.

To begin testing interactions, we also began to build sketch models. Making real, physical things allowed us to really test some of the interactions we were thinking about. The sketch models also allowed us to communicate iteractions within our team more easily.

Final Product

End result was a product called FightSafe, which puts force sensors into a boxers helmet. The referee of matches gets notified discretely when boxer is about to undergo irreparable head damage, and calls a technical knockout (which already exists) Boxers have access to the data collected in bouts at their local gyms, to encourage safer fighting techniques.
